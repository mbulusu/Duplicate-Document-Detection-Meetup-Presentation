Duplicate Document Detection with Python¶

If you ever worked with text documents, there is a high probability that youve had to deal with duplicate content at some point or the other. The process of manually scanning every document to detect duplicates is a tedious and, often, ineffective one.

This presentation focuses on designing a machine learning model that not only identifies similar documents based on their text similarity, but also utilizes the documents metadata to confirm that a pair of similar documents are indeed duplicates, thus reducing the need for any manual intervention in detecting duplicate documents. The solution is a 2-step process which involves calculating the text similarity between two documents, and subjecting the pair to a classifier for a verdict.

A sneak-peek into the solution:

Text Similarity - Candidate Duplicates When a new document enters the workflow, a cosine similarity score is calculated between the new document and every other document in the corpus. Document pairs that have similarity scores greater than a set threshold are filtered as Candidate Duplicates. This filtering means that we are ready to accept that all the pairs that have similarity scores lesser than our set similarity threshold are not duplicates.

Classifier These candidate duplicates will now be subjected to a trained classifier which will classify pairs of documents as duplicates based on their metadata features. Our classifier is a decision tree classification model that is trained on labeled duplicate documents and labeled non-duplicate documents, along with their corresponding similarity scores and metadata features like the document lengths, filing dates, document types etc.

The main reason behind using a classifier layer on top of cosine similarity scores is the way in which some press release documents/ mandatory disclosures/ news articles etc are filed. Documents which need to be filed every month/quarter under a specific format with minor changes in data, cannot be considered as duplicates, although they might have high text similarity. We call such documents - High Similarity Non Duplicates.

Through our 2-step process, let us see how we can identify such high similarity non duplicates along with duplicate documents.

Glossary of Terms:¶


Document: A news article.
Corpus: A collection of documents (news articles).
TFIDF Score: A statistic that determines how important a word is to a particular document when compared against several documents in the corpus.
Corpus TFIDF Matrix: A matrix of TFIDF scores for every document against every word in the corpus.
TFIDF Model: A TFIDF model trained on all the words from the corpus - which can be used later to calculate TFIDF scores for test documents against all the words from the corpus.
Test TFIDF Matrix: A matrix of TFIDF scores calculated for test documents against all the words from the corpus using the TFIDF (trained) model.
Cosine Similarity / Similarity Score: A score that measures similarity between two vectors (TFIDF vectors in this case) by taking the cosine of the angle between those vectors. Cosine similarity between the Corpus TFIDF Matrix and the Test TFIDF Matrix means that the similarity scores are calculated between the every document (vector) from the test documents and every document (vector) of the corpus.
Similarity Threshold: A cut off on similarity score which is calculated as the 5th percentile of the similarity score from labeled duplicate pairs of documents.
Candidate Duplicates: Pairs of documents that have similarity scores greater than the similarity threshold. These pairs are subjected to the classifier to determine definite duplicates.
Classifier: A decision tree classification model that is trained on labeled duplicate document pairs and labeled non-duplicate document pairs along with other metadata features.
High Similarity Non-duplicates: Pairs of documents that have high similarity scores (similar enough to be duplicates) but are not considered duplicates because they have been filed too far apart (different quarters).

However, if you care about the document metadata, consider pairs of documents that have cosine similarity scores of y or above as candidate duplicates, and proceed to the next step.

Filter Candidate Duplicates¶

Instead of subjecting all pairs of articles to the classifier, we filter a set of candidate duplicates using a similarity threshold. Similarity threshold is calculated as the 5% th percentile from the similarity scores of labeled duplicate articles. This means that only 5% of all labeled duplicate articles have similarity scores below the similarity threshold.

By filtering candidate duplicates from a wider set of articles, we are reducing the burden on the classifier. Here, the number of combinations of articles has reduce from 3454000 to 22 after filtering for candidate duplicates. Setting such a threshold means that we are ready to lose 5% of duplicate articles without detecting them.
